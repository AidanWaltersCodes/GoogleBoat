Google Boat

Your smart companion for safe and efficient navigation

About

The inspiration behind Google Boat is creating a similar map service for mobile users that Google Map provides, but in the context of commercial boating. There are recent trends of boating increasing in popularity with 40% of boat sales increasing during the Covid pandemic, and now 11.9% of U.S. households own a recreational boat. Because of the increase in demand, Google wants to give users the ability to know where boats are in recreational waters, how to easily navigate with your boat, and provide additional information that could be pertinent to all different types of boaters! With Google Boat users will be able to see the current water traffic occurring nearby to make informed decisions on where they should go and what docks they should use. Google boat, will use satellites to collect geospatial information to create water maps for users, determine weather patterns, potential obstacles, etc. Overall, Google Boat will enhance boaters' experience by helping them decide where to take their boat and how to navigate safely while on waters.

This app is designed for both recreational boaters and fishermen. Some of the key features in this app include real-time positioning, AI-assisted navigation, personalized boat management tools, and crowdsourced reporting of unsafe areas. The application uses advanced technologies like GPS, real-time data processing, and AI. Additionally, the app will run on a modular microservice architecture with Kubernetes for high availability and scalability. Google Boat will enhance the boating experience by providing users with useful information, optimized navigation routes, and promoting safety on the water.

Use Cases

Use Case 1: Novice Fisherman

John is a 28-year-old fisher who recently purchased his first boat. John wants to explore new fishing spots but is unsure about the best locations and how to navigate them properly. John discovers Google Boat and finds that it will help him carry out his fishing goals while still being a beginner.

When John opens the app, he is greeted with a user-friendly interface that displays a map. He can easily search for popular fishing spots and view information about different things happening in the area (weather, boat traffic, etc.). The app also provides John with AI-assisted navigation, helping direct him to his chosen location while considering everything going on in the area.

While on the water, John is happy being able to see the app’s real-time updates on wind speed, wave heights, and tide information, helping him make informed decisions while being new to fishing. John can easily make his way to his chosen fishing spot while saving his information on how to get there, as well as how to get back.

Use Case 2: Experienced Recreational Boater

Sarah is a 45-year-old teacher who has been enjoying recreational boating for many years. She owns a mid-sized motorboat and takes her family on many trips during weekend getaways. As someone who is experienced with boating, she is mainly concerned with planning efficient routes to save time and fuel costs. Sarah also wants to make sure that she gets reminders of when she needs to get maintenance checks on her boat so she can take care of her boat.

Sarah also likes the app’s ability to display the location of other boats in the area. The crowdsourced information from the app helps her make choices about which areas to visit and when. When exploring new boating spots, Sarah uses the Google Boat search functionality to find popular places to check out.

In both use cases, Google Boat proves to be a useful and easy-to-use solution, helping each user’s specific needs. By providing a range of features that help with safety, efficiency, and overall enjoyment. Google Boat establishes itself as a necessary tool for boaters and fishers of all skill levels.

Requirements

Functional Requirements

FR-1. The mobile application must have a map.

FR-2. The app must use GPS (Global Position System) to map current boat location and location of other boats.

FR-3. The app must have a map layer of waterways (lakes and oceans) and nearby land (determine mile radius).

FR-4. The app must ingest wind conditions and have a map layer to be added on top of waterways.

FR-5. The app must ingest tide and wave conditions and have a map layer to be added on top of waterways.

FR-6. The app must give ability for users to report areas of concern (debris, person overboard, etc).

FR-7. The app must prompt users if to provide their location and share that location with other users of the application.

FR-8. The app must have a search feature to look up waterways (lakes and oceans) and land destinations (docks and harbors).

FR-9. The app must provide navigation to other destinations (docks, harbors, or open water).

FR-10. For open water destinations, the app must prompt user distance (in nautical miles) they’d like to travel.

FR-11. For land destinations, the app must provide a fasted path through the waterways. The app must use weather, tide, and other vessels on water to create optimal path.

FR-12. The app must have a customizable checklist feature where the user can provide a list of items that must be complete before starting a navigation.

FR-13. The app must provide a feature for the user to provide their own boat information called “My Boat” (including where it’s docked, home dock is an option).

FR-14. The app must use boat information, weight, hull type, engine, to inform navigation in predicting length of navigation and gas consumed during trip.

FR-15. The app will provide generic notifications on normal maintenance checks on parts of the boats.

FR-16. The app must have an AI assisted feature to provide also provide suggested path through waterways for both water and land destinations; the suggested path will provide user if they’d like to maximize on fasted path or varying speeds of travel on certain paths to lower gas consumption.

FR-17. The app must save the location you departed from your navigation and use this location (if the user doesn’t override) to navigate back (implies can already start calculating the path back to make it faster).

FR-18. The map must be grayed out if the user is not within range of water.

FR-19. The map must have compass.

Non-Functional Requirements

NFR-1. The app must be available on Android Studio and the Apple Store.

NFR-2. The app must respond in x time for locations in a 20-mile radius (implies caching).

NFR-3. The app must respond in y time for locations outside of 20-mile radius (implies needing to grab from central database).

NFR-4. The app must provide navigation path to destination in a maximum 2 minutes, however a navigation path back from the destination in 20 seconds (can start calculations ahead of time since most likely the user will be returning on where they start).

NFR-5. The app must prompt all users if they want to share their geolocation and notify the app will have limited features without it.

NFR-6. The app must notify user to rely on boat’s technology during the navigation for the most accurate data.

NFR-7. All data to the app must be encrypted.

NFR-8. The backend servers must be running in Kubernetes to provide high availability to users (microservice architecture).

High Level Design

After reviewing the use cases and requirements, it was important to think about how the design of this project will work. It became clear that attempting to use a single architectural pattern across the system would not be beneficial, so we aimed to decompose the problem into more manageable pieces.

The first category encompasses real-time position tracking and vessel monitoring. This includes immediate processing and response times, handling constant streams of GPS data and position updates for multiple boats on the water. The systems need to track the user’s boat and maintain awareness of other boats in the area. This category requires event-driven architecture for the best performance.

The second category involves navigation and path optimization. This domain focuses on calculations for route planning, looking at different things such as weather, fuel efficiency, and personal boat specs. These components require computational resources but operate on a request response basis. Navigation information will be cached to the user and saved on the application to ensure no loss of navigation if internet service is lost.

The third category is mapping layer management and environmental data processing. This domain handles the integration and visualization of different data streams. The data streams this domain handles include depth information, weather conditions, and wave/tide data. These components need to process and combine different data sources in real-time while keeping optimal system performance.

The fourth category focuses on personal boat management and user data. Boat specifications, maintenance schedules, and user preferences are what this domain focuses on. Data persistence and storage are important as well as periodic updates.

By breaking our problem down into these four categories, we determined that a microservice architecture running on Kubernetes will provide the necessary flexibility and scalability for our project. Additionally, this will allow each component to use appropriate architectures for its specific requirements. The high-level module view of our design is shown below in Figure X.

DIAGRAM

We will structure the software system that implements these categories of requirements into four main modules, each encapsulated within the microservice architecture. The Real-Time Positioning Module handles continuous position tracking and vessel monitoring with an event-driven architecture. Then, the Navigation Module will manage pathfinding with computational services. The Mapping Layers Module looks at and combines different environmental and geographical data. Finally, the Personal Boat Module handles everything needed for the user’s boat like maintenance and boat specs. All of these modules are managed and coordinated through Kubernetes. Having container orchestration will ensure high availability, and efficient resource utilization.
